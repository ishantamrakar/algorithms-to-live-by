Optimal Stopping

Options arrive one at a time and you can't go back. If you know how many there are and can rank them, the optimal strategy is the 37% rule: spend the first 37% just looking, build a benchmark, then take the next option that beats it.

The Math

Strategy
Look at the first $r$ options without choosing. Then pick the next option that beats the best seen so far. Never go back.
Breaking it into cases

The best candidate sits at some position $k$. To succeed, two things must happen simultaneously — so we multiply their probabilities:

  • The best is not in the look phase: $k > r$, so you don't skip them. Since the best is equally likely to be anywhere, $$P(\text{best at position } k) = \frac{1}{n}$$
  • No false leap before position $k$: nobody between positions $r{+}1$ and $k{-}1$ triggers your rule. This only fails if some runner-up appears before position $k$ but after position $r$. That runner-up is safe only if the second-best among the first $k{-}1$ candidates fell inside your look phase. There are $r$ safe slots out of $k{-}1$ total, so $$P(\text{no false leap}) = \frac{r}{k-1}$$

Both must hold at the same time, and they are independent, so multiply:

$$P(\text{success} \mid \text{best at } k) \cdot P(\text{best at } k) = \frac{1}{n} \cdot \frac{r}{k-1}$$
Total probability
Sum over every position where success is possible ($k = r{+}1$ to $n$). Cases $k \leq r$ contribute zero because you skip the best during the look phase. $$P(r,\, n) \;=\; \sum_{k=r+1}^{n} \frac{1}{n} \cdot \frac{r}{k-1} \;=\; \frac{r}{n} \sum_{k=r+1}^{n} \frac{1}{k-1}$$
Continuous approximation
Let $x = r/n$ be the look fraction. As $n \to \infty$ the sum becomes an integral: $$\frac{r}{n}\sum_{k=r+1}^{n}\frac{1}{k-1} \;\longrightarrow\; x\int_{x}^{1}\frac{1}{t}\,dt \;=\; -x\ln x$$ So the continuous approximation is simply $$P(x) = -x \ln x$$
Finding the optimum
Differentiate and set to zero: $$\frac{dP}{dx} = -\ln x - 1 = 0 \;\implies\; x = \frac{1}{e} \approx 0.368$$ Look at the first $1/e \approx 37\%$ of options, then leap at the next best. The maximum probability of success also converges to $$P\!\left(\tfrac{1}{e}\right) = -\frac{1}{e}\ln\frac{1}{e} = \frac{1}{e} \approx 36.8\%$$
